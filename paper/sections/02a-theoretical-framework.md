# 2.1 Theoretical Framework

## 2.1.1 Transit Equity and the Need-Supply Gap

The analytical foundation of transit equity research rests on a deceptively simple premise: public transit service should be distributed in proportion to the population's need for it. When it is not, a spatial gap emerges between where transit is provided and where it is most required. Currie (2010) formalized this premise into a quantitative framework by constructing parallel indices of transit supply and social need in Melbourne, then overlaying them spatially to identify areas where the two diverge. His finding that outer suburbs received 75 percent less transit service than inner areas despite exhibiting the highest social need established the need-supply gap as an empirical phenomenon rather than an assumption. The framework demonstrated that spatial mismatch between transit provision and transit dependence is systematic, not incidental, and that it follows a predictable geographic pattern: investment concentrates in the urban core while need accumulates at the periphery.

Mamun and Lownes (2011a) advanced this framework by developing a composite Transit Need Index (TNI) from socioeconomic and demographic indicators, including poverty rate, zero-vehicle household percentage, and the share of elderly and youth populations captured through a dependency ratio. Their approach shifted the operationalization of "need" from a single deprivation measure to a multi-dimensional construct, recognizing that transit dependence arises from the intersection of income poverty, vehicle inaccessibility, and demographic vulnerability. The TNI methodology, demonstrated in Hartford, Connecticut, provided a reproducible template for constructing need indices from census-type data in any metropolitan context where such variables are available.

Jiao and Dillivan (2013) extended the gap framework by coining the term "transit desert" to describe areas where transit-dependent populations exceed the transit service available to them. Applying GIS-based overlay analysis across Charlotte, Chicago, Cincinnati, and Portland, they quantified demand-supply gaps and mapped transit deserts as discrete spatial zones. The transit desert concept moved the discourse from continuous gap scores to categorical classification, making the analysis more legible for planners and policymakers who need to identify priority zones for intervention rather than interpret gradient maps.

Jomehpour and Smith-Colin (2020) subsequently refined the transit desert methodology by integrating social vulnerability indicators and opportunity access metrics into the supply-demand comparison. Their equity-focused revision recognized that not all unserved areas carry equal urgency: a low-density exurban zone with poor transit but high car ownership is qualitatively different from a dense, low-income neighborhood with identical transit scores but no private mobility alternatives. By weighting the gap analysis toward populations with fewer alternatives, Jomehpour and Smith-Colin brought the framework closer to an equity instrument rather than a purely spatial diagnostic.

The evolution from Currie's binary gap identification to Jomehpour and Smith-Colin's vulnerability-weighted analysis traces a clear trajectory: transit equity measurement has moved from asking "where is transit missing?" to asking "where is transit missing for people who cannot substitute private transport?" This trajectory informs the present research directly. The two-axis Transit Equity Matrix constructed here positions a Transit Need Index against a Transit Accessibility Index, classifying each spatial unit into one of four quadrants. The Q4 quadrant, termed "High Need, Low Access," operationalizes Jiao and Dillivan's transit desert concept within a continuous scoring framework, while the quadrant structure as a whole extends Currie's spatial overlay into a classification system that distinguishes between underservice (Q4), overservice relative to need (Q2), adequate matching (Q1), and low-priority zones (Q3). The TNI itself draws on Mamun and Lownes's indicator selection logic, adapted to the Indonesian statistical landscape with population density and inverted household expenditure replacing variables unavailable in the BPS census.

## 2.1.2 Distributional Equity Measurement

Gap analysis identifies where mismatches occur, but it does not quantify how unequal the overall distribution of transit accessibility is across a metropolitan area. A city could have several transit deserts yet still exhibit moderate overall inequality if those deserts are small and the remainder of the population is relatively well-served. Conversely, a city with no discrete desert zones could still exhibit extreme inequality if accessibility is concentrated among a narrow band of affluent, centrally located residents. Distributional equity measures address this limitation by summarizing the entire accessibility distribution into a single metric.

Delmelle and Casas (2012) brought this approach to the transit equity literature by applying Gini coefficients and Lorenz curves to the distribution of BRT accessibility across Cali, Colombia. The Gini coefficient, borrowed from income inequality measurement, captures the degree to which transit accessibility deviates from a perfectly equal distribution: a Gini of zero indicates every spatial unit has identical accessibility, while a Gini approaching one indicates all accessibility is concentrated in a single unit. In Cali, Delmelle and Casas found significant spatial inequity in the MIO BRT system across income groups, demonstrating that the Gini framework is both applicable and informative in developing-country settings where transit networks are still expanding and socioeconomic stratification is pronounced. Their work established the Gini coefficient as a standard summary metric for transit equity, complementing the spatially explicit but location-specific information provided by gap analysis.

Pereira, Banister, Schwanen, and Wessel (2019) deepened this distributional approach in Rio de Janeiro by examining how transit investments for the 2014 World Cup and 2016 Olympics altered the accessibility distribution across income groups. Their before-and-after analysis revealed that substantial infrastructure spending generated larger accessibility benefits for higher-income groups, and that subsequent service cuts disproportionately penalized low-income populations. The Rio case demonstrated a critical insight: transit investment alone does not guarantee equity improvement. The distributional effects depend on where and how service is provided relative to where vulnerable populations live. Pereira et al.'s methodology, which combined multimodal routing with income-stratified accessibility distributions, showed that the Gini coefficient can serve as both a diagnostic tool and an evaluation metric for policy interventions.

These two strands of measurement, gap analysis and distributional equity, are complementary rather than competing. Gap analysis reveals the spatial structure of inequity: which neighborhoods are underserved, where transit deserts concentrate, and how they relate to demographic patterns. Distributional metrics reveal the magnitude of inequity: how far the metropolitan system deviates from an equitable allocation and whether interventions reduce or exacerbate that deviation. The present research adopts both approaches. The quadrant classification identifies transit deserts and their spatial patterns (testing H1), while the Gini coefficient and Lorenz curve quantify the overall equity of accessibility distribution at both kelurahan and H3 resolutions (testing H2). The what-if scenario simulation uses the Gini delta as its primary outcome metric, measuring whether a hypothetical intervention in a Q4 zone produces a larger equity improvement than the same intervention in a Q2 zone (testing H3).

## 2.1.3 The Modifiable Areal Unit Problem in Transit Equity

Any spatial analysis that aggregates point-level phenomena into areal units confronts the Modifiable Areal Unit Problem (MAUP), first formalized by Openshaw (1984). MAUP describes a fundamental sensitivity: statistical results change when the same underlying data is aggregated into different spatial configurations, whether those configurations differ in scale (the number of units), shape (the boundary geometry), or both. In transit equity research, MAUP means that conclusions about which areas are underserved may depend as much on the spatial unit chosen for analysis as on the actual distribution of transit service.

Javanmard, Lee, Kim, Liu, and Diab (2023) provided the most direct empirical demonstration of MAUP effects in transit equity analysis. Using bus on-time performance data from Winnipeg, they compared social equity assessments computed at the stop level, the route level, and the neighborhood level. Their central finding is that the spatial scale at which equity is measured substantively changes the conclusion: route-level analysis suggested equitable service distribution, while stop-level and neighborhood-level analyses revealed significant inequities. The same data, the same equity metric, and the same city produced contradictory equity diagnoses depending solely on the aggregation unit. This result carries direct implications for policy, because planners typically work with administrative boundaries that may mask fine-grained inequity visible at smaller scales.

The MAUP concern is particularly acute in metropolitan regions where administrative boundaries vary dramatically in size. In Jabodetabek, kelurahan areas range from approximately 0.5 km^2 in dense central Jakarta to over 50 km^2 in the outer kabupaten of Bogor and Tangerang, a hundred-fold variation. A large suburban kelurahan may contain both well-served corridors adjacent to a KRL station and deeply underserved interior zones several kilometers from any transit stop. When TNI and TAI scores are computed at the kelurahan level, these internal extremes average into a moderate composite score that hides both the transit-accessible corridor and the transit desert interior. The resulting equity assessment is an artifact of boundary geometry rather than a reflection of lived access.

Hexagonal grid systems offer a partial remedy. The H3 indexing system, developed for spatial analytics applications, provides uniform hexagonal cells at multiple resolutions. At resolution 8, each hexagon covers approximately 0.74 km^2, roughly equivalent to a walkable neighborhood of 860 meters edge-to-edge. Because all hexagons have the same area and a compact shape with minimal edge effects, they reduce the scale and zonation components of MAUP simultaneously. A single large kelurahan that would receive one averaged score is instead decomposed into dozens of hexagons, each scored independently, allowing within-unit variation to surface in the analysis.

No existing study systematically compares the same transit equity framework applied at two resolutions, one administrative and one uniform, in a single metropolitan area. Javanmard et al. (2023) demonstrated that scale matters but did not propose a resolution that eliminates the problem; they compared three pre-existing aggregation levels rather than testing a purpose-built uniform grid. The dual-resolution design adopted here directly tests whether the MAUP effects documented in Winnipeg manifest in Jabodetabek and, if so, whether they lead to substantively different equity diagnoses when kelurahan boundaries are replaced by H3 hexagons. The comparison is quantified through a confusion matrix of quadrant classifications, Cohen's kappa statistic, and the percentage of spatial units reclassified between the two resolutions.

## 2.1.4 Generalized Cost Models for Multi-Modal Comparison

The generalized cost framework is a standard instrument in transport economics for comparing the full burden of a trip across modes. A generalized cost function aggregates monetary costs (fares, fuel, tolls, parking), time costs (monetized through a value of time parameter), and disutility components (transfer penalties, discomfort, fatigue) into a single monetary equivalent that represents the total "price" a traveler pays for a given trip by a given mode. The framework enables mode comparison on a common scale: the mode with the lowest generalized cost is the rational choice for a utility-maximizing traveler, holding preferences constant.

In transit equity research, generalized cost has been used primarily in a binary form: transit versus car. This binary framing reflects the mode choice landscape of North American, European, and Australian cities where the foundational transit equity literature was produced. When Currie (2010) identified spatial gaps in Melbourne or Delmelle and Casas (2012) measured accessibility inequality in Cali, the implicit counterfactual was that populations without adequate transit would drive or be immobile. The motorcycle, where it appeared at all, was treated as a niche mode rather than a systemic competitor.

In Southeast Asia, this framing is inadequate. Ng (2018) examined urban transportation mode choice across five Southeast Asian cities, including Jakarta, Bangkok, Manila, Kuala Lumpur, and Ho Chi Minh City, finding that motorcycle dominance is driven by three structural advantages: low operating cost (fuel consumption roughly one-fifth that of a car per kilometer), flexibility in congested traffic (lane-splitting and use of narrow streets inaccessible to cars), and the ability to provide door-to-door service that eliminates the first-mile and last-mile penalties inherent in transit trips. In Jakarta specifically, motorcycles account for the largest mode share of daily trips, and their competitive position is reinforced by an Indonesian regulation prohibiting motorcycles from toll roads, which paradoxically reduces their monetary cost for suburban trips while increasing travel time only modestly on congested surface roads.

Sukor and Bhayo (2024) investigated the behavioral dimension of the motorcycle-transit competition, studying the factors that influence motorcycle riders to switch to public transit across developing Southeast Asian cities. Their analysis identified first-mile and last-mile quality and fare affordability as the strongest predictors of modal shift. Riders were willing to switch when transit offered a walkable, connected first-mile experience and when the total fare remained below a threshold roughly equivalent to motorcycle operating costs for the same trip. For longer trips exceeding thirty minutes by motorcycle, physical fatigue from heat, rain, and exhaust exposure emerged as an additional push factor toward transit, suggesting that generalized cost models should incorporate a fatigue premium for motorcycle trips that increases with ride duration.

These findings motivate a three-way generalized cost model that computes costs for transit, car, and motorcycle independently, then evaluates transit competitiveness against the cheapest private alternative rather than against car alone. The competitive dynamics are distance-dependent in ways that a binary model cannot capture. For suburban origins twenty to forty kilometers from the CBD, car is typically the most expensive option due to toll and parking costs, motorcycle occupies a middle position that is cheap in monetary terms but physically taxing for long rides, and transit is cheapest if first-mile connections function adequately. For near-CBD origins of three to eight kilometers, the motorcycle dominates because fuel cost is negligible, no tolls apply, and door-to-door travel time undercuts transit's access and egress overhead. The present research integrates this three-way comparison as Layer 5 of the Transit Accessibility Index, expressing the result as a transit competitive ratio against the minimum private cost. This extension addresses a gap in the transit equity literature, where no prior framework has embedded a three-mode generalized cost comparison within a composite accessibility index designed for equity diagnosis.

## 2.1.5 First-Mile and Last-Mile Connectivity

The quality of pedestrian access to transit stops and stations mediates the relationship between transit supply and actual usability. A rail station with high-frequency service and affordable fares produces limited accessibility gains if the surrounding road network lacks sidewalks, pedestrian crossings, or feeder services that connect residents to the station. This first-mile problem is well-documented in the transport planning literature as a principal barrier to transit ridership, and it carries particular weight in equity analysis because populations with the highest transit need, those without private vehicles, are precisely those most affected by poor pedestrian connectivity.

Hardi and Murad (2023) provided the most direct evidence of this problem in the Jakarta context. Their spatial accessibility analysis of the TransJakarta BRT system found that only 41 percent of Jakarta's road network provides adequate pedestrian access to BRT stops, and that 58 percent of BRT stations suffer from poor connectivity to the surrounding street network. Using walk-time isochrones at five, ten, and fifteen-minute thresholds, they demonstrated that physical proximity to a BRT stop, measured in Euclidean distance, systematically overstates actual accessibility when the pedestrian network is fragmented by highways, canals, or gated housing compounds that force lengthy detours. The Jakarta finding aligns with a broader pattern in rapidly urbanizing cities where transit infrastructure has been built for vehicle throughput rather than pedestrian access, creating a paradox in which transit investment fails to translate into transit accessibility for nearby residents.

The first-mile deficit interacts with the generalized cost framework in a specific way. When first-mile connectivity is poor, travelers face a choice: walk a long, uncomfortable route to the transit stop, or pay for a motorcycle taxi (ojek or ride-hailing) to bridge the gap. Either option adds cost to the transit trip, whether in time (monetized through the value of time) or in money (the ojek fare). This additional cost erodes transit's competitiveness against door-to-door private modes, particularly the motorcycle, which eliminates the first-mile entirely. Sukor and Bhayo (2024) confirmed this mechanism, finding that first-mile quality is the strongest predictor of motorcycle-to-transit modal shift: when the walk to the station is short, safe, and shaded, riders switch; when it is not, they ride.

The present research addresses first-mile connectivity through Layer 1 of the Transit Accessibility Index, which combines network walking distance to the nearest stop, pedestrian road share, intersection density as a measure of network connectivity, and the availability of feeder services. This layer ensures that a spatial unit adjacent to a high-frequency rail station does not receive an inflated accessibility score if the pedestrian infrastructure connecting residents to that station is deficient. The design responds directly to the evidence from Hardi and Murad (2023) that Euclidean proximity is an unreliable proxy for actual first-mile access in Jakarta, and to the finding from Sukor and Bhayo (2024) that first-mile quality is the decisive factor in the transit-versus-motorcycle competition that defines mode choice in Jabodetabek.
