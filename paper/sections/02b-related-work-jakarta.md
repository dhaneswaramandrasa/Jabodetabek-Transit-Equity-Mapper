# 2.2 Related Work: Transit Equity in Jakarta and Jabodetabek

## 2.2.1 Jakarta's Multi-Modal Transit System

The Jakarta metropolitan area, commonly referred to as Jabodetabek (an acronym for Jakarta, Bogor, Depok, Tangerang, and Bekasi), is home to over 30 million residents spread across a politically fragmented region governed by multiple autonomous municipalities. DKI Jakarta, the special capital region, sits at the center of this conurbation, surrounded by a suburban ring of satellite cities (kota) and regencies (kabupaten) that house the majority of the region's commuting workforce. This governance fragmentation --- with DKI Jakarta, Kota Bogor, Kabupaten Bogor, Kota Depok, Kota Tangerang, Kabupaten Tangerang, Kota Tangerang Selatan, Kota Bekasi, and Kabupaten Bekasi each maintaining separate transport planning authority --- presents a structural barrier to integrated metropolitan transit provision.

The formal public transit network serving Jabodetabek has expanded in the past two decades but remains concentrated within DKI Jakarta's administrative boundaries. TransJakarta, launched in 2004 as the first bus rapid transit system in Southeast Asia, now operates approximately 260 routes with dedicated corridors and feeder services across the capital. The KRL Commuterline, a suburban rail network inherited from the national railway and operated by KAI Commuter, provides the primary rail spine linking Jakarta to the Bodetabek periphery through approximately 80 stations across six lines. The MRT Jakarta, operational since 2019, runs a single 16-kilometer north-south corridor with 13 stations, while the LRT Jabodebek, completed in 2023, connects East Jakarta to Bekasi and Cibubur through 18 stations. JakLingko and Mikrotrans feeder services provide last-mile connections within DKI Jakarta, though their coverage beyond the capital's borders is limited.

This modal hierarchy creates a pronounced spatial asymmetry. Rail-based services (MRT, LRT, KRL) offer high capacity and speed but serve fixed corridors. BRT provides broader spatial coverage but operates predominantly within DKI Jakarta. Feeder networks bridge the gap between trunk services and neighborhoods, yet their reach thins at the municipal boundary. The result is that residents of suburban Bodetabek --- where the population has grown most rapidly --- face the longest first-mile distances to any formal transit stop, the fewest modal options, and the most transfer-intensive journeys to reach employment centers in central Jakarta. This spatial structure makes Jabodetabek a particularly instructive case for transit equity analysis: the question is not merely whether transit exists, but whether it reaches the populations that depend on it most.

## 2.2.2 Transit Accessibility Research in Jakarta

Despite its scale, Jabodetabek has received limited attention in the transit equity literature. The two most relevant studies --- Hardi and Murad (2023) and Taki, Maatouk, and Qurnfulah (2018) --- address accessibility within Jakarta but do not frame their analyses in terms of equity gaps between transit supply and socioeconomic need.

Hardi and Murad (2023) conducted a GIS-based spatial accessibility analysis of the TransJakarta BRT system using walk-time isochrones at 5, 10, and 15-minute thresholds. Their findings were sobering: only 41% of Jakarta's road network fell within adequate walking distance of a BRT stop, and 58% of BRT stations were classified as poorly connected to the surrounding pedestrian network. These results quantify a first-mile walkability deficit that undermines the BRT system's theoretical coverage. The study demonstrates that stop density alone is an insufficient indicator of accessibility --- the quality of pedestrian infrastructure linking residents to stops is a binding constraint on actual transit use.

Taki et al. (2018) took a different approach, evaluating transit-oriented development (TOD) potential across the Jakarta Metropolitan Region using GIS-based multi-criteria analysis. Their framework combined transit proximity, population density, and land use mix to classify areas by TOD readiness. While the study provided a spatial typology of development potential around existing transit nodes, it was oriented toward land use planning rather than equity diagnosis. The analysis identified where TOD is feasible, but not where transit service is most critically absent relative to the population's need for it.

Both studies share three limitations that define the gap this research addresses. First, neither constructs a composite need-versus-access framework: Hardi and Murad measure accessibility in isolation from socioeconomic need, while Taki et al. assess development potential without reference to transit-dependent populations. Second, neither study extends its analysis beyond DKI Jakarta to cover the full Jabodetabek metropolitan region. The Bodetabek periphery, where commuters face the longest and most costly journeys, is excluded entirely. Third, neither applies distributional equity metrics --- Gini coefficients, Lorenz curves, or spatial autocorrelation measures --- that would quantify how unequally transit accessibility is distributed across income groups or geographies. The Jakarta transit literature, in short, tells us about the system's spatial reach but not about whether that reach aligns with where it is most needed.

## 2.2.3 The Suburban Bodetabek Gap

The omission of Bodetabek from existing transit research is consequential because the suburban ring is precisely where transit poverty is most acute. Population growth in Jabodetabek over the past three decades has been suburban-led: the satellite cities of Bekasi, Tangerang, and Depok have grown far more rapidly than central Jakarta, which is approaching residential saturation. These suburban populations generate millions of daily commuting trips toward employment concentrations in the Sudirman--Thamrin central business district and surrounding commercial zones, yet the transit infrastructure serving these corridors remains thin relative to demand.

The KRL Commuterline provides the backbone of suburban transit connectivity, but its stations are spaced several kilometers apart and concentrated along radial rail corridors, leaving vast areas between lines without rail access. TransJakarta BRT coverage drops off sharply at the DKI Jakarta boundary, and while some feeder services extend into Bodetabek, their frequency and reliability fall below levels that would make them viable alternatives to private transport. The LRT Jabodebek partially addresses this gap along the Bekasi and Cibubur corridors, but its 18-station network covers only a narrow band of the eastern periphery.

The institutional dimension compounds the infrastructure deficit. Each municipality in the Bodetabek ring maintains its own transport planning authority, and no single agency has jurisdiction over the full metropolitan transit network. Badan Pengelola Transportasi Jabodetabek (BPTJ), the regional transport coordination body, has a mandate to integrate cross-boundary planning, but the operational reality is one of fragmented decision-making. This fragmentation means that transit investment decisions are made at the municipal level, favoring projects within jurisdictional boundaries over the cross-boundary connections that suburban commuters actually need. A transit equity analysis that stops at DKI Jakarta's border misses the population for whom equity gaps are largest.

No existing study covers the full Jabodetabek metropolitan region with a composite need-access framework that spans all eight municipal jurisdictions. The kelurahan (village-level administrative unit) provides a policy-relevant spatial unit that is consistent across the region, with approximately 1,800 units ranging from dense urban wards of 0.5 km^2 to sprawling suburban villages exceeding 50 km^2. This 100-fold variation in area is itself a methodological concern --- the subject of Section 2.1's discussion of the Modifiable Areal Unit Problem --- but the kelurahan remains the finest administrative grain at which socioeconomic data is available and planning decisions are made. Extending the analysis from DKI Jakarta's roughly 270 kelurahan to the full region's 1,800 is a prerequisite for any equity assessment that claims to represent Jabodetabek's transit reality.

## 2.2.4 Motorcycle Dominance and Three-Way Mode Competition

Transit equity frameworks developed in Western and Australian contexts operate on a binary mode choice assumption: residents either take transit or drive a car. In Jabodetabek, this assumption is fundamentally misaligned with how people actually move. The motorcycle is not a niche mode but the dominant form of private transport, and for a large share of the population, the relevant decision is not "transit or car" but "transit or motorcycle."

Ng (2018) examined urban transportation mode choice across five Southeast Asian cities --- Jakarta, Bangkok, Manila, Kuala Lumpur, and Ho Chi Minh City --- and found that motorcycle dominance in the region is driven by three reinforcing factors: low operating cost (fuel consumption roughly Rp 400/km in Indonesian terms, compared to approximately Rp 1,000/km for cars), superior maneuverability in congested traffic, and the ability to provide flexible door-to-door service that compensates for poor transit first- and last-mile connectivity. The study established that motorcycles are not merely a mode of last resort for the poor; they are a rational choice across a wide income band given the prevailing transit service quality in Southeast Asian cities.

The competitive dynamics between transit and motorcycle are distance-dependent, creating a spatial pattern that has direct implications for equity analysis. For suburban origins 20--40 km from the CBD, car travel is most expensive due to toll and parking costs, motorcycle travel is moderately expensive but physically taxing over long distances, and transit is the cheapest option if first- and last-mile connections function adequately. For near-CBD origins 3--8 km from the center, motorcycle dominates: fuel costs are negligible, there are no tolls (motorcycles are prohibited from Indonesian toll roads), parking fees are minimal, and door-to-door travel time undercuts transit's access and egress overhead. This distance-dependent competitive structure means that transit's ability to attract riders varies spatially in ways that a simple proximity-based accessibility index cannot capture.

Sukor and Bhayo (2024) investigated the factors driving modal switch from motorcycles to public transport across developing Southeast Asian cities. Their behavioral analysis identified first/last-mile quality and fare affordability as the two strongest predictors of modal shift. Riders who face long, uncomfortable, or unsafe walks to transit stops are unlikely to abandon their motorcycles regardless of the trunk service's speed or frequency. This finding aligns with the spatial accessibility deficit that Hardi and Murad (2023) documented for TransJakarta: the 58% of BRT stations with poor pedestrian connectivity are, in Sukor and Bhayo's framing, stations that cannot compete with the motorcycle's door-to-door convenience.

The implication for transit equity measurement is that any accessibility index applied in Jabodetabek must account for motorcycle competitiveness as a contextual factor. A spatial unit may score well on transit proximity and service frequency, yet its residents may still depend on motorcycles because the generalized cost of the full transit trip --- including the time and discomfort of first-mile access, transfers, and last-mile egress --- exceeds the motorcycle alternative. Western frameworks that omit this mode from the cost comparison systematically overestimate transit's effective accessibility in motorcycle-dominated cities. This gap motivates the three-way generalized cost model embedded as Layer 5 of our Transit Accessibility Index, which computes transit's cost competitiveness against both car and motorcycle to produce a transit competitiveness ratio that reflects the actual mode choice landscape of Jabodetabek commuters.
