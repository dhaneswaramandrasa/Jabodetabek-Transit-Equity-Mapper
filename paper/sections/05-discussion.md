# 5. Discussion

This study set out to answer one foundational question about Jabodetabek's transit system: does the spatial distribution of transit access align with the socioeconomic distribution of transit need, and does the answer change depending on how the spatial units of analysis are drawn? The empirical work produced three categories of findings that address this question directly: a spatial distribution of misalignment that confirms the suburban periphery's disadvantage (H1), a quantified resolution effect showing that administrative units suppress equity gaps that hexagonal units expose (H2), and a scenario simulation demonstrating that the quadrant framework identifies high-leverage intervention points (H3). This section discusses the implications of each finding, situates the results within the broader literature, considers the study's limitations, and identifies directions for future research.

## 5.1 H1 --- Spatial Mismatch: Peripheral Concentration of Transit Deserts

The spatial distribution of Q4 (High Need, Low Access) zones confirms H1: transit deserts are disproportionately concentrated in the Bodetabek periphery, while central Jakarta exhibits the inverse pattern --- relatively low need paired with comparatively high access (Q2). This outcome is consistent with the pattern documented by Andani et al. (2025), whose kecamatan-level job accessibility analysis in DKI Jakarta found that public transit accessibility is substantially lower in peripheral, low-density areas --- a pattern the present analysis extends to the full Bodetabek ring. The LISA cluster analysis identifies statistically significant Q4 clusters in the eastern Bekasi corridor, the southern Bogor fringe, and several dense kampung areas in southern Tangerang --- zones characterised by high population density, low household expenditure, and the absence of formal KRL or BRT service within walkable distance.

The Q2 (Low Need, High Access) concentration in central Jakarta is equally consistent with the literature. Hardi and Murad (2023) documented TransJakarta BRT walkable accessibility primarily serving the inner Jakarta core, while Taki et al. (2018) showed that BRT catchment zones defined by walk isochrones are biased toward high-density, centrally located neighbourhoods. The Layer 1 (First-Mile Quality) scores confirm this pattern: the highest first-mile accessibility scores are concentrated in the Jakarta Pusat and Jakarta Selatan districts where the TransJakarta corridor network is densest and pedestrian infrastructure is most complete.

The spatial structure of this mismatch, however, reveals a more nuanced geography than a simple core-periphery divide. Q4 zones are not uniformly distributed across the Bodetabek ring: they cluster along specific radial corridors --- the Bekasi eastern corridor, the Depok southern corridor, and outer Tangerang --- where residential density is high (driven by relatively recent housing development) but no formal transit investment has followed. The continuous Equity Gap Score surface provides spatial granularity beyond the categorical quadrant classification. Zones with Equity Gap scores exceeding two standard deviations above the metropolitan mean identify the planning authority's most urgent spatial targets.

A notable finding is that within DKI Jakarta itself, Q4 zones are localised rather than absent. These intra-Jakarta transit deserts appear in dense, low-income kampung areas where transit infrastructure nominally exists but is physically inaccessible --- consistent with Hardi and Murad's (2023) finding that 58% of TransJakarta stations lack adequate pedestrian connectivity to surrounding road networks. The TAI Layer 1 analysis explains this: the limiting factor in these zones is not transit absence but walk infrastructure deficit, supporting the conclusion that pedestrian infrastructure investment may yield higher equity returns per rupiah than new transit construction in intra-Jakarta Q4 zones.

## 5.2 H2 --- Resolution Effect: What Kelurahan Analysis Conceals

The resolution comparison is the study's primary methodological contribution, and the results confirm H2 with direct implications for how equity analyses should be conducted in heterogeneous metropolitan contexts. The Gini coefficient computed over H3 hexagonal units exceeds the Gini computed over kelurahan, a finding consistent with Javanmard et al.'s (2023) empirical prediction that finer and more uniform spatial units reveal greater inequality. The direction and magnitude of this difference are analytically significant: the kelurahan-level Gini underestimates metropolitan inequity because large suburban administrative units average away the internal variation that H3 hexagons expose.

The Cohen's kappa statistic comparing quadrant classifications across the two unit systems falls below conventional thresholds for substantial agreement in the Bodetabek suburban zones --- the zones where kelurahan areas are largest and most heterogeneous. This disagreement is not random but systematic: the kelurahan classification tends toward Q3 (Low Need, Low Access) or Q1 (Well-Served) in areas where H3 disaggregation reveals Q4 clusters. The direction of the MAUP bias is as predicted by H2: large administrative units average away the Q4 signal by blending transit-deprived core areas with transit-accessible peripheral portions of the same administrative unit. A substantial proportion of spatial units are classified differently under the two unit systems, meaning that the actionable output of the equity analysis --- the list of zones requiring intervention --- depends on which unit of analysis is chosen. These misclassifications are not merely academic: they determine which communities are visible to planners as transit-deprived.

This finding has direct implications for the transit equity literature. Studies that report equity conclusions at administrative resolution --- including Andani et al. (2025) at kecamatan level and most North American transit equity studies at the census tract level --- should be interpreted as potential underestimates of spatial inequality in heterogeneous metropolitan areas. The equal-area hexagonal grid does not introduce a correction so much as a sensitivity test: it asks whether the equity conclusions are robust to the aggregation choice, and in Jabodetabek, they are not. The practical recommendation follows: equity studies in heterogeneous metropolitan contexts should report Gini coefficients and spatial clustering statistics at both administrative and H3 resolution, treating the difference between the two as a measure of the MAUP's empirical impact.

Gelb and Alizadeh (2025) provide an important reinforcing finding from a complementary methodological angle: they demonstrated that classical Gini coefficients applied to overall accessibility distributions miss group-specific deprivation, with concentration curves revealing 12--19% lower median accessibility for vulnerable populations invisible to aggregate Gini. The quadrant framework developed here is, in effect, a vertical equity instrument targeting the worst-off --- providing the complementary diagnostic that Gelb and Alizadeh recommend. The resolution comparison extends their critique further: just as a single equity metric can obscure group-specific deprivation, a single spatial resolution can obscure unit-specific deprivation. The combination of their multi-metric critique and the dual-resolution critique developed here points toward a methodological standard for transit equity analysis: report both group-disaggregated equity metrics and dual-resolution spatial statistics to ensure that equity findings are not artefacts of either metric choice or spatial unit choice.

## 5.3 H3 --- Scenario Validation: The Quadrant as a Prioritisation Tool

The counterfactual simulation confirms H3: placing a new transit node in a Q4 zone (High Need, Low Access) produces a larger delta in the Equity Gap Score --- and a larger reduction in the regional Q4 count --- than the same intervention placed in a Q1 or Q2 zone. This result validates the quadrant framework's core prioritisation claim: classifying spatial units by need-access alignment, rather than by accessibility alone, identifies where infrastructure investment yields the greatest equity return. An intervention in a high-accessibility area improves absolute accessibility for already-served populations but does little to reduce regional equity gaps; an intervention in a transit desert improves access for those who need it most.

This finding connects to the broader literature on transit investment evaluation. Pereira et al. (2019) showed that transit investments in Rio de Janeiro disproportionately benefited higher-income groups, but their analysis could not prospectively identify the alternative investment locations that would have reversed this pattern. The counterfactual simulation developed here addresses this gap by providing a reproducible methodology for testing intervention equity impact prospectively: before committing to a new corridor or station, planners can quantify the equity impact by simulating the intervention in the model and observing the resulting shift in Q4 count and Equity Gap distribution.

The single-node simulation design is deliberately conservative: it tests whether the Q4 classification correctly identifies high-equity-return locations, not whether any specific network expansion design is optimal. The finding that Q4-targeted nodes consistently outperform Q1/Q2-targeted nodes in equity impact is therefore a statement about the framework's discriminatory validity, not a specific infrastructure recommendation. Future work could extend the simulation to corridor-level or multi-node scenarios, but the single-node design is sufficient for demonstrating the framework's capacity to distinguish between high- and low-return intervention locations.

## 5.4 Three-Mode Generalised Cost Model Insights

The three-mode generalised cost model (Layer 5 of the TAI) produces an analytically important signal. Zones where the transit-to-motorcycle fare differential is large and the transit travel time advantage is small are precisely those where even the most transit-deprived populations will rationally choose the motorcycle. As Ng (2018) demonstrated, the transit-to-motorcycle fare ratio is a dominant predictor of modal shift in Southeast Asian cities; a two-mode model that excludes motorcycle cost would overestimate transit's competitive accessibility in zones where the motorcycle is the practical default mode. Sukor and Bhayo (2024) further established that first/last-mile quality and fare affordability are the strongest predictors of motorcycle-to-transit modal shift, lending empirical support to the combined influence of Layer 1 (First-Mile Quality) and Layer 5 (Cost Competitiveness) in driving TAI scores in Bodetabek peripheral zones.

This finding suggests that fare interventions --- not just network expansion --- belong in the transit equity toolkit. In zones where the fare parity gap is the binding constraint rather than network coverage, subsidised transit fares may deliver greater equity returns per rupiah than infrastructure investment. Studies from Western frameworks that compare only transit versus car may systematically overestimate transit accessibility in Southeast Asian contexts where the motorcycle is the practical default.

## 5.5 Methodological Implications

Three methodological implications emerge from the combined findings.

First, the MAUP effect in transit equity analysis is empirically consequential in heterogeneous metropolitan contexts and should be treated as a standard methodological sensitivity check rather than a theoretical footnote. The H3 hexagonal grid offers a practical, open-source, globally consistent alternative unit for this check. The overhead of running a dual-resolution analysis is modest; the informational value is substantial.

Second, the five-layer TAI structure --- disaggregating transit accessibility into first-mile, service quality, CBD journey chain, last-mile, and generalised cost components --- reveals that different layers bind in different zones. In intra-Jakarta Q4 zones, Layer 1 (First-Mile Quality) is the binding constraint; in Bodetabek peripheral zones, Layer 3 (CBD Journey Chain) and Layer 5 (Cost Competitiveness) drive the low TAI scores. This layer-specific diagnostic enables more targeted interventions than a single composite accessibility score would permit.

Third, the three-mode generalised cost model is methodologically necessary for accurate accessibility assessment in the Jabodetabek context. A two-mode model that excludes motorcycle cost would overestimate transit's competitive position in precisely the zones where motorcycle dependence is highest and the equity case for intervention is strongest.

## 5.6 Planning Implications for Jabodetabek

For Jabodetabek transport policy, the findings support a three-part spatial investment strategy. First, new formal transit investment --- stations, corridors, feeder services --- should be prioritised in the Q4 clusters along the eastern Bekasi, southern Depok, and outer Tangerang corridors, where the Equity Gap Score and Q4 concentration are highest. Second, in intra-Jakarta Q4 zones where transit infrastructure exists but pedestrian access is poor, pedestrian connectivity improvements (footways, crossings, feeder service densification) should take priority over new station construction. Third, fare subsidy mechanisms warrant consideration in high-need zones where the transit-to-motorcycle generalised cost differential, rather than network absence, is the primary driver of low transit uptake.

These priorities are consistent with the findings of Andani et al. (2025), who similarly concluded that Bodetabek periphery deprivation is the region's primary equity challenge and that transit investment in DKI Jakarta has reached a stage of diminishing marginal equity returns. The present analysis adds spatial precision to this conclusion by identifying the specific kelurahan and H3 zones with the highest Equity Gap Scores, and by demonstrating through the scenario simulation that these zones are also the highest-equity-return intervention locations.

The layer-specific diagnostics further refine the planning response. The finding that Layer 1 (First-Mile Quality) is the binding constraint in intra-Jakarta Q4 zones, while Layer 3 (CBD Journey Chain) and Layer 5 (Cost Competitiveness) drive deprivation in peripheral Bodetabek, implies that a uniform policy response across the metropolitan area would be suboptimal. Different zones require different interventions: pedestrian infrastructure in the inner city, network expansion in the periphery, and fare reform in zones where cost competitiveness is the binding constraint.

## 5.7 Limitations

Four limitations bound the interpretation of these findings. First, the analysis is a static snapshot; the GTFS data and BPS demographic indicators reflect a single time period, and equity conclusions will change as the network evolves. The LRT Jabodebek, inaugurated in 2023, is incorporated in the GTFS feed used, but post-2023 route expansions are not.

Second, several TNI indicators rely on BPS synthetic small-area estimates at kelurahan level; these estimates carry uncertainty that the aggregate TNI inherits. Sensitivity analysis shows that broad spatial patterns are robust to weight perturbations, but individual kelurahan scores should be interpreted as estimates with confidence bounds rather than exact measurements.

Third, the informal transit sector --- ojek, angkot, and chartered minibuses --- is unrepresented in the GTFS feed and excluded from the TAI. In areas where informal transit provides the primary first- and last-mile connection to formal stops, the TAI underestimates effective accessibility. This exclusion is particularly consequential in Bodetabek peripheral zones where informal transport may partially compensate for the absence of formal service.

Fourth, the study is descriptive and exploratory; causality between transit investment patterns and observed equity outcomes cannot be established from the cross-sectional design employed here.

## 5.8 Generalisability and Future Research

The methodological framework developed here is designed to be transferable. The TNI--TAI structure uses data types --- census demographics, GTFS feeds, OpenStreetMap road networks --- available for most metropolitan regions globally. The H3 hexagonal grid is universally applicable, and the generalised cost layer requires only fare schedules and mode-specific travel time estimates. The pipeline is parameterised for alternative GTFS feeds, enabling adaptation to other Southeast Asian megacities (Metro Manila, Bangkok, Ho Chi Minh City, Kuala Lumpur) that share the combination of formal transit expansion and motorcycle-dominant transport cultures that make Jabodetabek a representative rather than exceptional case.

Future research should address the limitations identified above. Real-time GTFS integration would enable temporal variation analysis. Incorporation of informal transport networks --- via GPS trace data or household travel surveys --- would produce a more complete picture of effective accessibility. Longitudinal analysis, comparing equity distributions before and after specific transit investments, would enable causal inference about the equity impacts of infrastructure decisions. Finally, extending the what-if simulation to corridor-level and multi-node scenarios would increase its planning utility, though at the cost of greater computational complexity and stronger assumptions about network effects.
