# 2.3 Methodological Precedents

## 2.3.1 Multimodal Routing and Travel Time Computation

Transit accessibility measurement has undergone a fundamental shift from proximity-based metrics toward realistic travel time computation that accounts for the full complexity of multimodal journeys. Early approaches relied on Euclidean or network distance to the nearest transit stop as a proxy for accessibility, an approach that captures physical proximity but ignores the combinatorial reality of public transit travel: waiting for a vehicle, transferring between lines, and walking to and from stations at both ends of the journey. The development of schedule-aware routing algorithms has made it possible to model these journey chains at metropolitan scale.

The R5 routing engine, developed by Conveyal, implements the RAPTOR (Round-bAsed Public Transit Optimized Router) algorithm for multimodal path search. Rather than computing a single shortest path, RAPTOR evaluates all Pareto-optimal journey options across a departure time window, accounting for walking access and egress legs, in-vehicle travel on one or more transit lines, and transfer waiting times between services. Pereira, Saraiva, Herszenhut, Braga, and Conway (2021) introduced r5r as an R-language interface to the R5 engine, demonstrating its capacity to compute origin-destination travel time matrices and cumulative accessibility measures for the Sao Paulo metropolitan area using GTFS schedule data and OpenStreetMap road networks as inputs. Their benchmarking showed that R5 produces travel time matrices orders of magnitude faster than traditional graph-based routing, enabling analyses that cover entire metropolitan regions rather than sampled corridors.

Fink, Klumpenhouwer, Saraiva, Pereira, and Tenkanen (2022) subsequently developed r5py, a Python wrapper for the same R5 engine that provides GeoPandas-compatible outputs and parallel processing capabilities. The Python ecosystem integration is significant because it allows seamless connection to spatial analysis libraries (geopandas, PySAL, h3-py) and data science workflows without language switching. For the present research, r5py serves as the computational engine for TAI Layer 3 (CBD journey chain), the dominant component of the Transit Accessibility Index. The tool ingests GTFS feeds for all formal transit modes in Jabodetabek — KRL Commuterline, MRT Jakarta, TransJakarta BRT, LRT Jabodebek, and Mikrotrans feeders — alongside the OpenStreetMap road network, and computes realistic door-to-door travel times with departure time sampling across the morning peak window (07:00--08:00). This departure window approach captures the effect of service frequency variation: a station served by trains every five minutes yields consistently short travel times across sampled departures, while a station served every fifteen minutes produces higher variance and a worse median outcome.

The choice of r5py over alternative routing tools reflects three practical considerations for the Jabodetabek context. First, the RAPTOR algorithm handles the region's complex transfer patterns — a commuter from Bogor may take KRL to Manggarai, transfer to MRT to Dukuh Atas, then walk to the Sudirman office district — as a single optimized journey chain rather than requiring manual specification of transfer points. Second, GTFS is the standard data format for the transit agencies operating in the region, and R5 natively consumes GTFS alongside OSM without preprocessing. Third, the gravity-weighted CBD model used in this research (which weights travel times to nine CBD zones with Sudirman--Thamrin receiving five times the weight of satellite centers) requires computing travel times from every spatial unit to multiple destinations, a task that benefits from R5's batch matrix computation.

## 2.3.2 Composite Index Construction

The construction of composite indices for transit accessibility and transit need draws on a well-established tradition in transport geography. Mamun and Lownes (2011b) proposed a composite index of public transit accessibility that integrates three measurement perspectives — developer, planner, and operator — into a single standardized score. Their approach normalizes individual accessibility components (proximity to stops, route coverage, service frequency) onto a common grading scale and combines them through weighted linear aggregation. The multi-perspective integration ensures that the resulting index captures not just one dimension of accessibility but the interplay between physical proximity, service provision, and network coverage. Applied to Meriden, Connecticut, the composite index revealed substantial within-city variation that no single-indicator measure could detect.

Adapting composite index methods to data-constrained environments requires attention to indicator availability and normalization robustness. Rathod, Joshi, and Arkatkar (2025) addressed this challenge by developing a Composite Accessibility Index designed for developing-country contexts where validated GTFS feeds may be incomplete and census data may be available only at coarse spatial granularity. Their framework encompasses proximity, coverage, frequency, and connectivity dimensions while acknowledging that some indicators must be approximated or derived from secondary sources. This precedent is directly relevant to Jabodetabek, where BPS socioeconomic data is published at the kecamatan level rather than the kelurahan level, and where the LRT Jabodebek lacks a validated GTFS feed.

Two methodological choices in composite index construction merit discussion: normalization and weighting. Min-max normalization, which rescales each indicator to the [0, 1] interval, is the dominant approach in the transit accessibility literature because it preserves the relative ordering of spatial units while making indicators commensurable (Mamun & Lownes, 2011b; Rathod et al., 2025). A known vulnerability of min-max normalization is sensitivity to extreme values: a single spatial unit with an anomalously high population density can compress the scores of all other units toward zero. Winsorization at the 2nd and 98th percentiles — capping values beyond these thresholds before normalization — mitigates this distortion without discarding observations entirely.

On weighting, the transit equity literature offers no consensus on optimal weight assignment. Mamun and Lownes (2011a) adopted equal weights across their Transit Need Index indicators, arguing that in the absence of empirical evidence favoring one indicator over another, equal weighting is the least arbitrary default. Rathod et al. (2025) similarly used equal weights in their composite framework. The present research follows this convention for the TNI (five indicators at 0.20 each) while adopting differentiated weights for the TAI layers based on the journey chain logic: the CBD journey chain (Layer 3) receives the highest weight (0.35) because it captures the core commuting accessibility that drives transit use decisions in Jabodetabek, while supporting layers receive lower weights (0.15--0.20). To guard against weight sensitivity, the methodology includes Monte Carlo perturbation analysis (1,000 iterations with plus or minus 20 percent random variation on each weight) and PCA-derived weights as a robustness check.

## 2.3.3 H3 Hexagonal Grids and Dasymetric Disaggregation

The choice of spatial unit shapes the results of any areal analysis, a problem formalized by Openshaw (1984) as the Modifiable Areal Unit Problem. Administrative boundaries — kelurahan in the Indonesian context — are drawn for governance purposes, not analytical precision. In Jabodetabek, kelurahan areas range from 0.5 square kilometers in dense central Jakarta to over 50 square kilometers in the rural fringes of Kabupaten Bogor, a hundredfold variation that introduces systematic aggregation bias. A large suburban kelurahan containing both a well-served transit corridor and a deeply underserved interior will average to a moderate equity score that conceals both extremes.

The H3 hexagonal grid system, developed by Uber Technologies, provides a hierarchical spatial indexing framework with uniform cell areas at each resolution level. At resolution 8, each hexagonal cell covers approximately 0.74 square kilometers with an edge-to-edge diameter of roughly 860 meters — a scale that approximates a walkable neighborhood. Three properties of H3 hexagons make them attractive for transit equity analysis. First, constant area eliminates the size-dependent aggregation bias inherent in variable-area administrative units. Second, hexagons have consistent adjacency: every cell borders exactly six neighbors at equal distances, simplifying spatial autocorrelation analysis and neighbor-weight matrix construction. Third, the hierarchical nesting of H3 resolutions allows sensitivity testing: the same analysis can be repeated at resolution 7 (approximately 5.2 square kilometers per cell) and resolution 9 (approximately 0.11 square kilometers) to evaluate whether equity conclusions are stable across scales.

Populating H3 hexagons with socioeconomic data that is collected at administrative boundaries requires disaggregation. Dasymetric mapping, an approach with roots in cartographic tradition and formalized for population estimation by Mennis (2003) and extended to gridded population products by Tatem (2017), uses an ancillary variable — typically a high-resolution population raster — to allocate aggregate counts to finer spatial units in proportion to estimated population density. In this research, the WorldPop gridded population dataset (approximately 100-meter resolution) serves as the allocation surface. BPS kecamatan-level socioeconomic indicators (poverty rate, household expenditure, zero-vehicle household percentage, dependency ratio) are redistributed to H3 cells using population-weighted dasymetric allocation: each hexagon receives a share of the parent administrative unit's indicator value proportional to its share of that unit's population as estimated by WorldPop. Road network indicators are derived by clipping OSM segments to hexagon boundaries and computing length totals within each cell; transit stops are assigned by point-in-polygon spatial join; and travel times are computed directly from hexagon centroids via r5py.

This dual-resolution design — applying the same equity framework at both kelurahan and H3 resolution 8 — responds directly to the empirical finding of Javanmard, Lee, Kim, Liu, and Diab (2023) that the spatial scale of analysis substantively changes transit equity conclusions. Their comparison of stop-level, route-level, and neighborhood-level assessments of bus service reliability in Winnipeg showed that route-level aggregation masked inequities visible at finer scales. The present research extends this logic to a developing-country metropolitan context where administrative units are far more heterogeneous in size than the Canadian census tracts used by Javanmard et al., and where the analytical alternative is not simply a finer administrative tier but a fundamentally different spatial framework — uniform hexagons versus variable polygons.

## 2.3.4 Spatial Equity Metrics

Measuring transit equity requires metrics that capture both the overall distribution of accessibility across a metropolitan area and the spatial structure of that distribution. Three complementary approaches, each grounded in the transit equity literature, are combined in this research: distributional metrics (Gini coefficient and Lorenz curve), spatial clustering statistics (Global and Local Moran's I), and categorical classification (quadrant framework).

The Gini coefficient, borrowed from income inequality analysis, summarizes the degree of inequality in transit accessibility distribution as a single value between zero (perfect equality) and one (maximum concentration). Delmelle and Casas (2012) applied this approach to evaluate the spatial equity of BRT-based accessibility in Cali, Colombia, demonstrating that the Gini coefficient is both interpretable and informative in a developing-country transit context. The accompanying Lorenz curve provides a visual representation: the diagonal represents perfect equity (every spatial unit has equal accessibility), and the area between the curve and the diagonal corresponds to the Gini value. Pereira, Banister, Schwanen, and Wessel (2019) extended this framework in Rio de Janeiro by computing Gini coefficients before and after transit investments, showing that infrastructure expansion does not automatically reduce inequality — the distributional effect depends on where and for whom accessibility improves. In the present research, Gini coefficients are computed at both kelurahan and H3 resolutions, and the difference between the two Gini values serves as one indicator of the MAUP effect predicted by H2.

Distributional metrics, however, are aspatial: the Gini coefficient treats each spatial unit as an independent observation without regard to its location or neighbors. Spatial autocorrelation statistics address this gap. Global Moran's I tests whether the overall spatial pattern of a variable (such as TAI scores) exhibits clustering, randomness, or dispersion across the study area. Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA), the local decomposition of Moran's I, identify statistically significant clusters and outliers at the individual unit level: High-High clusters (hot spots of high accessibility surrounded by high accessibility), Low-Low clusters (cold spots), and High-Low or Low-High spatial outliers. For kelurahan-level analysis, queen contiguity defines the spatial weights matrix (two kelurahan are neighbors if they share a boundary or vertex); for H3 hexagons, a k-nearest-neighbor matrix with k equal to six exploits the regular adjacency structure of the hexagonal grid. LISA maps reveal whether Q4 transit desert zones form spatially contiguous corridors — a pattern with different policy implications than scattered, isolated pockets of underservice.

The quadrant classification framework provides a categorical overlay that translates continuous TNI and TAI scores into actionable policy categories. Currie (2010) pioneered the two-axis structure by plotting transit supply against social need in Melbourne, identifying areas of spatial mismatch. Jiao and Dillivan (2013) formalized the demand-supply gap as the transit desert concept, focusing on the quadrant where high demand meets low supply. In the present research, spatial units are classified into four quadrants by median-splitting on both the TNI and TAI axes: Q1 (Well-Served: high need met by high access), Q2 (Low Need, High Access: potential overinvestment), Q3 (Low Priority: low need, low access), and Q4 (Transit Desert: high need, low access). The quadrant framework complements rather than replaces the distributional and spatial metrics: the Gini coefficient describes overall inequality, LISA identifies where clusters form, and the quadrant map shows which clusters represent policy-urgent mismatches between need and supply.

## 2.3.5 Synthesis: What This Research Extends

Each methodological component described above has established precedent in the transit equity literature. The contribution of the present research lies not in inventing new techniques but in combining and extending them in ways that address the four gaps identified in the literature review.

From the composite index tradition (Mamun & Lownes, 2011b; Rathod et al., 2025), this research borrows the principle of multi-indicator aggregation with min-max normalization and weighted linear combination. It extends this tradition by restructuring the Transit Accessibility Index as a five-layer journey chain model — first-mile quality, service quality, CBD journey chain, last-mile quality, and cost competitiveness — that reflects the sequential decision structure of the Jabodetabek commuter experience rather than treating accessibility dimensions as interchangeable inputs to a flat composite.

From the multimodal routing tools (Pereira et al., 2021; Fink et al., 2022), this research borrows the RAPTOR-based travel time computation with GTFS and OSM inputs, applying it at metropolitan scale across the full Jabodetabek region. It extends the standard accessibility computation by embedding the r5py output within a gravity-weighted CBD model where travel times to nine employment centers are differentially weighted, producing a single journey chain score (TAI Layer 3) that captures the polycentric but Sudirman-dominant employment geography of the region.

From the spatial equity measurement tradition (Delmelle & Casas, 2012; Pereira et al., 2019; Currie, 2010; Jiao & Dillivan, 2013), this research borrows the Gini coefficient, Lorenz curve, and quadrant classification framework. It extends these by computing all equity metrics at two spatial resolutions simultaneously, enabling a direct test of whether administrative boundaries mask equity gaps that uniform hexagons expose — a question raised by the MAUP literature (Javanmard et al., 2023) but not previously tested through a full dual-resolution equity framework in a single metropolitan area.

From the Southeast Asian mobility literature (Ng, 2018; Sukor & Bhayo, 2024), this research borrows the empirical observation that the motorcycle, not the car, is the marginal competitor to transit in cities like Jakarta. It extends this insight by operationalizing it as TAI Layer 5: a three-way generalized cost model that computes transit's cost competitiveness against both car and motorcycle, with distance-dependent fatigue premiums and the Indonesian toll road motorcycle prohibition reflected in the cost parameters. No prior transit equity framework has integrated mode-competitive cost analysis as a layer within a composite accessibility index.

The what-if scenario simulation — placing a hypothetical transit station in a Q4 transit desert zone and measuring the resulting shift in quadrant membership, Gini coefficient, and affected population — has no direct methodological antecedent in the equity literature. It borrows catchment analysis methods (buffer and isochrone computation) from accessibility research and applies them within the equity quadrant framework to test whether the framework functions as a prioritization tool, not merely a diagnostic one.
